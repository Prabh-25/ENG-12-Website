About PTSD


Posttraumatic stress disorder (PTSD) is a mental disorder which occurs when someone might have experienced or witnessed a traumatic event or series of events. An individual may experience this as emotionally or physically harmful or life-threatening and may affect mental, physical and social well-being. Experiences like natural disasters, accidents, terrorist acts, war/combat, rape/sexual assault, domestic violence and bullying are the most common reasons triggering the trauma. The trauma was discovered in the combat zones first and was started getting recognition by military in 1980. There were different terms used to describe it earlier like shell shock for WW1 veterans, but the trauma itself was a controversial thing to study and discuss in the psychiatric theory.

War PTSD


Specifically talking about War PTSD, it is usually suffered by soldiers and veterans during combat. During war exposure to life threatening situations, witnessing the bloodshed, or losing the fellow soldiers are the main reasons behind the trauma. According to statistics 10-30% of WW2 veterans returned home with PTSD, which numbered in hundred-thousands. According to another report around 21.8% of veterans who served in Iraq and Afghanistan which were around 61,000 suffered from PTSD. Another reason behind why soldiers suffer from PTSD is the combat guilt, which is either killing others or witnessing the killing of innocents, fellow comrades or even enemies.

Symptoms

A person suffering from War PTSD, can have go through a range of symptoms. The most common one is when being threatened about their life, because of the things they have witnessed during combat.Usually this includes shell shock, trauma from mortar shells, other explosives and loud firing sounds. Also during combat if the soldier is unable to save the fellow comrades, then that guilt inside slowly turns into trauma and is experienced in form of nightmares. The person also gets stuck in the cycle of substance abuse, which also leads to other problems/symptoms like anger issues, psychosis (getting detached from the reality), difficulty in sleeping and even seizures, killing someone and suicide in extreme cases.

Treatment

The most effective way to treat PTSD is by a therapy called CBT(Cognitive Behavioral Therapy). This therapy focuses on changing the way how a person thinks, and shifting their negative thoughts to positive. It makes the patient believe that good things can happen in their life. There are several steps included in the therapy:-

• Reprocessing the traumatic events to help veterans face the memories safely.

• Pulling them out from their isolation, because while suffering from PTSD, the patient's mind considers them under threat all the time .

• Helping them manage their anger outbursts through training and breathing exercises.

• Improving their sleep pattern.

• The sessions can be one on one or in group, for peer support and help manage victims with similar experiences.
